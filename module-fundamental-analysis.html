Introduction to Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis is a method of evaluating a security's intrinsic value by examining related economic, financial, and other qualitative and quantitative factors. Unlike technical analysis, which focuses on price patterns, fundamental analysis seeks to determine what a company is actually worth.

Core Philosophy

  • Intrinsic Value - Every stock has an underlying true value
  • Market Inefficiency - Price can deviate from intrinsic value
  • Long-Term Perspective - Focus on business fundamentals over time
  • Value Investing - Buy undervalued stocks, sell overvalued ones

Fundamental vs Technical Analysis

  • Fundamental: What should the price be? (Business value)
  • Technical: What is the price doing? (Market psychology)

Key Factors Considered

  • Financial statements and ratios
  • Management quality and corporate governance
  • Industry trends and competitive position
  • Economic conditions and market environment
  • Growth prospects and future earnings potential

Understanding Financial Statements

Financial statements are the foundation of fundamental analysis. They provide a comprehensive view of a company's financial health and performance.

Income Statement

Shows a company's financial performance over a specific period. Also known as the Profit & Loss (P&L) statement.

SAMPLE INCOME STATEMENT
Revenue $1,000,000
Cost of Goods Sold ($600,000)
Gross Profit $400,000
Operating Expenses ($200,000)
Net Income $200,000

Balance Sheet

Provides a snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. Follows the accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

SAMPLE BALANCE SHEET
Cash & Equivalents $150,000
Accounts Receivable $100,000
Total Assets $500,000
Total Liabilities $200,000
Shareholders' Equity $300,000

Cash Flow Statement

Shows how changes in balance sheet accounts and income affect cash and cash equivalents. Breaks cash flow into three categories:

  • Operating Activities - Cash from main business operations
  • Investing Activities - Cash from buying/selling assets
  • Financing Activities - Cash from debt/equity transactions

Financial Ratios and Metrics

Financial ratios transform raw financial data into meaningful insights about a company's performance, efficiency, and financial health.

Profitability Ratios

  • Gross Profit Margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue
  • Net Profit Margin = Net Income / Revenue
  • Return on Assets (ROA) = Net Income / Total Assets
  • Return on Equity (ROE) = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity
  • Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) = NOPAT / Invested Capital

Liquidity Ratios

  • Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
  • Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities
  • Cash Ratio = Cash & Cash Equivalents / Current Liabilities

Solvency Ratios

  • Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity
  • Debt-to-Assets Ratio = Total Debt / Total Assets
  • Interest Coverage Ratio = EBIT / Interest Expense

Efficiency Ratios

  • Asset Turnover = Revenue / Total Assets
  • Inventory Turnover = COGS / Average Inventory
  • Days Sales Outstanding = (Accounts Receivable / Revenue) √ó 365

Valuation Ratios

  • Price-to-Earnings (P/E) = Stock Price / Earnings per Share
  • Price-to-Book (P/B) = Stock Price / Book Value per Share
  • Price-to-Sales (P/S) = Stock Price / Sales per Share
  • EV/EBITDA = Enterprise Value / EBITDA

Valuation Methods

Valuation is the process of determining a company's fair value or intrinsic value. Different methods provide different perspectives on what a company is worth.

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

The most comprehensive valuation method based on the principle that a company's value equals the present value of its future cash flows.

DCF Steps:

  • 1. Project future free cash flows (usually 5-10 years)
  • 2. Calculate terminal value (perpetual growth)
  • 3. Determine discount rate (WACC)
  • 4. Discount all cash flows to present value
  • 5. Subtract net debt to get equity value

Comparable Company Analysis

Values a company based on how similar companies are priced in the market.

  • Select Comparable Companies - Similar size, industry, growth
  • Calculate Multiples - P/E, EV/EBITDA, P/S, etc.
  • Apply Multiples - To target company's metrics
  • Range of Values - Consider multiple scenarios

Precedent Transactions Analysis

Values a company based on prices paid for similar companies in past acquisitions.

  • Control Premium - Buyers typically pay 20-40% above market price
  • Synergies - Strategic value to the acquirer
  • Market Conditions - Economic environment affects valuations

Asset-Based Valuation

Values a company based on the value of its assets, minus liabilities.

  • Book Value - Historical cost of assets
  • Liquidation Value - Value if assets sold quickly
  • Replacement Cost - Cost to replace assets

Economic and Industry Analysis

Company analysis must be placed in the context of the broader economy and industry. Even great companies can struggle in poor economic conditions.

Macroeconomic Factors

  • GDP Growth - Economic expansion drives corporate earnings
  • Interest Rates - Affect borrowing costs and investment returns
  • Inflation - Impacts costs, pricing power, and real returns
  • Employment - Consumer spending and business confidence
  • Consumer Confidence - Predicts future spending patterns

Industry Analysis Framework

Porter's Five Forces

  • Threat of New Entrants - Barriers to entry, economies of scale
  • Bargaining Power of Buyers - Customer concentration, price sensitivity
  • Bargaining Power of Suppliers - Supplier concentration, switching costs
  • Threat of Substitutes - Alternative products/services
  • Competitive Rivalry - Industry concentration, growth rates

Industry Life Cycle

  • Introduction - High risk, high growth potential
  • Growth - Rapid expansion, increasing competition
  • Maturity - Slower growth, stable profits
  • Decline - Falling demand, consolidation

Key Industry Metrics

  • Market Size and Growth Rate - Total addressable market
  • Profit Margins - Industry average profitability
  • Capital Intensity - Investment requirements
  • Cyclicality - Sensitivity to economic cycles

Quality Assessment

Beyond numbers, assessing the qualitative aspects of a business is crucial for long-term investment success.

Management Quality

  • Leadership Experience - Track record and industry expertise
  • Capital Allocation - How management uses company capital
  • Transparency - Clear communication with shareholders
  • Compensation Structure - Alignment with shareholder interests
  • Corporate Governance - Board independence and oversight

Competitive Advantages (Moats)

Durable competitive advantages protect profits from competition:

  • Brand Power - Strong customer loyalty and pricing power
  • Network Effects - Value increases with more users
  • Switching Costs - Difficult or expensive for customers to leave
  • Cost Advantages - Lower production or distribution costs
  • Regulatory Barriers - Licenses, patents, or regulatory approval

Business Model Analysis

  • Revenue Streams - How the company makes money
  • Customer Base - Concentration and loyalty
  • Pricing Power - Ability to raise prices without losing customers
  • Scalability - Ability to grow without proportional cost increases

Risk Factors

  • Business Risks - Competition, technology changes, regulation
  • Financial Risks - Debt levels, liquidity, currency exposure
  • Operational Risks - Supply chain, key person dependencies
  • Legal Risks - Lawsuits, regulatory actions

Integration and Investment Decision

Fundamental analysis culminates in making informed investment decisions by integrating all analysis components into a cohesive investment thesis.

Building an Investment Thesis

  • Business Understanding - What the company does and why it matters
  • Financial Health - Strong balance sheet and cash flow generation
  • Growth Catalysts - Drivers of future growth
  • Valuation - Attractive price relative to intrinsic value
  • Risk Assessment - Potential downsides and mitigation

Margin of Safety

Benjamin Graham's concept of buying at a discount to intrinsic value:

  • Purpose - Protection against analysis errors and bad luck
  • Typical Range - 20-50% below estimated intrinsic value
  • Higher Risk - Requires larger margin of safety
  • Lower Risk - Can accept smaller margin of safety

Investment Checklist

A systematic approach to ensure thorough analysis:

  • ‚òê Understand the business model completely
  • ‚òê Analyze financial statements for 5+ years
  • ‚òê Calculate and interpret key ratios
  • ‚òê Assess management quality and governance
  • ‚òê Evaluate competitive position and moats
  • ‚òê Conduct industry and economic analysis
  • ‚òê Perform valuation using multiple methods
  • ‚òê Identify key risks and catalysts
  • ‚òê Determine appropriate margin of safety
  • ‚òê Set price targets and exit criteria

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Confirmation Bias - Only seeking information that supports your thesis
  • Overconfidence - Believing your analysis is infallible
  • Anchoring - Being influenced by irrelevant reference points
  • Herding - Following popular opinion without independent analysis
  • Short-term Focus - Ignoring long-term fundamentals for short-term gains

Continuous Monitoring

Fundamental analysis is not a one-time event:

  • Quarterly Earnings - Review financial performance
  • Industry Trends - Monitor competitive landscape
  • Management Changes - Assess impact on strategy
  • Valuation Updates - Adjust targets as conditions change
