Introduction to Derivatives

Derivatives are financial contracts whose value derives from an underlying asset or benchmark. They are powerful tools for hedging risk, speculating on price movements, and gaining exposure to assets without owning them directly.

What Makes Derivatives Special?

  • Leverage - Control large positions with small capital
  • Risk Transfer - Shift risk from one party to another
  • Price Discovery - Reveal market expectations
  • Market Efficiency - Enable arbitrage and complete markets

Types of Derivatives

  • Options - Right, but not obligation, to buy/sell at set price
  • Futures - Obligation to buy/sell at future date, set price
  • Forwards - Customized futures contracts (OTC)
  • Swaps - Exchange cash flows based on different variables
  • Exotics - Complex, customized derivatives

Market Participants

  • Hedgers - Use derivatives to reduce risk
  • Speculators - Profit from price movements
  • Arbitrageurs - Exploit price inefficiencies
  • Market Makers - Provide liquidity

Risks and Considerations

  • Complexity requires deep understanding
  • Leverage amplifies both gains and losses
  • Counterparty risk in OTC derivatives
  • Liquidity risk in less common contracts

02

Options Fundamentals

Options give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a specified price on or before a specific date. They are the most versatile derivative instruments.

Call Options

  • Definition - Right to BUY at strike price
  • Profit When - Underlying price > Strike + Premium
  • Maximum Loss - Premium paid
  • Maximum Gain - Unlimited

Put Options

  • Definition - Right to SELL at strike price
  • Profit When - Strike price > Underlying + Premium
  • Maximum Loss - Premium paid
  • Maximum Gain - Strike price - Premium

Key Terminology

  • Strike Price - Price at which option can be exercised
  • Expiration Date - Date option expires
  • Premium - Price paid for the option
  • In-the-Money - Option has intrinsic value
  • Out-of-the-Money - Option has no intrinsic value
  • At-the-Money - Strike equals current price
CALL OPTION PAYOFF
Profit/Loss increases as underlying price rises above strike

Option Styles

  • American - Can be exercised anytime before expiration
  • European - Can only be exercised at expiration
  • Bermudan - Can be exercised on specific dates

Options Pricing Models

Options pricing is complex due to multiple factors. The Black-Scholes model revolutionized options trading by providing a mathematical framework for valuing options.

Factors Affecting Option Prices

  • Underlying Price - Higher price increases call value, decreases put value
  • Strike Price - Higher strike decreases call value, increases put value
  • Time to Expiration - More time increases option value
  • Volatility - Higher volatility increases option value
  • Risk-Free Rate - Higher rates increase call value, decrease put value
  • Dividends - Higher dividends decrease call value, increase put value

Black-Scholes Model

The groundbreaking formula for European options:

  • Assumes constant volatility and risk-free rate
  • No dividends (original model)
  • Efficient markets with no transaction costs
  • Lognormally distributed returns

The Greeks

Greeks measure sensitivity of option prices to various factors:

Greek Measures Impact
Delta (Œî) Price sensitivity Change in option price per $1 change in underlying
Gamma (Œì) Delta sensitivity Rate of change of delta
Theta (Œò) Time decay Loss of option value per day

Implied Volatility

  • Definition - Market's expectation of future volatility
  • Calculation - Reverse-engineered from option prices
  • IV Rank - Current IV relative to historical range
  • Volatility Smile - Pattern of IV across strike prices

Basic Options Strategies

Options can be combined to create strategies with specific risk-reward profiles. Understanding these strategies is essential for effective options trading.

Bullish Strategies

Long Call
Buy call option, limited risk, unlimited upside
Risk: Premium Paid
Bull Call Spread
Buy lower strike call, sell higher strike call
Risk: Net Debit
Bull Put Spread
Sell higher strike put, buy lower strike put
Risk: Margin Requirement

Bearish Strategies

Long Put
Buy put option, limited risk, large downside potential
Risk: Premium Paid
Bear Put Spread
Buy higher strike put, sell lower strike put
Risk: Net Debit
Bear Call Spread
Sell lower strike call, buy higher strike call
Risk: Margin Requirement

Neutral Strategies

Iron Condor
Combine bull put and bear call spreads
Risk: Margin Requirement
Iron Butterfly
All options at same strike price
Risk: Margin Requirement
Straddle
Buy call and put at same strike
Risk: Total Premium

Strategy Selection Factors

  • Market Outlook - Bullish, bearish, or neutral
  • Volatility Expectation - Rising, falling, or stable
  • Risk Tolerance - Conservative vs aggressive
  • Capital Requirements - Available margin and premium
  • Time Horizon - Short-term vs long-term strategies

Advanced Options Strategies

Advanced strategies combine multiple options to create sophisticated payoff structures for specific market conditions and risk management needs.

Volatility Strategies

Long Straddle
Buy call and put at same strike, profit from large moves
Risk: Total Premium
Long Strangle
Buy out-of-the-money call and put
Risk: Total Premium
Ratio Spreads
Unequal numbers of long and short options
Risk: Unlimited

Income Generation

Covered Call
Own stock, sell call against it
Risk: Stock Ownership
Cash-Secured Put
Sell put, have cash to buy stock if assigned
Risk: Stock Purchase
Calendar Spread
Sell near-term, buy far-term options
Risk: Net Debit

Hedging Strategies

Protective Collar
Own stock, buy put, sell call
Risk: Limited Upside
Protective Put
Own stock, buy put as insurance
Risk: Premium Cost

  • Condor Spreads - Four-strike strategies for range-bound markets
  • Diagonal Spreads - Different strikes and expirations
  • Ladder Spreads - Multiple strike prices with increasing risk

Strategy Management

  • Adjustment Techniques - Rolling, closing, adding legs

Futures and Forwards

Futures and forwards are contracts obligating parties to buy/sell assets at predetermined prices on future dates. They're essential for commodities, currencies, and interest rate markets.

Futures vs Forwards

  • Standardization - Futures are standardized, forwards are customized
  • Trading Venue - Futures trade on exchanges, forwards are OTC
  • Settlement - Futures settle daily, forwards at maturity
  • Counterparty Risk - Futures have clearing house, forwards have counterparty risk

Futures Contract Specifications

  • Contract Size - Standardized quantity of underlying
  • Delivery Months - Specific months when contracts expire
  • Tick Size - Minimum price movement
  • Settlement Method - Physical delivery or cash settlement

Types of Futures

  • Commodity Futures - Agricultural, energy, metals
  • Financial Futures - Stock indices, currencies, interest rates
  • Weather Futures - Temperature, rainfall, snowfall
  • Real Estate Futures - Property price indices

Pricing Futures

Futures price = Spot price + Cost of carry

  • Cost of Carry = Storage costs + Financing costs - Convenience yield - Dividends
  • Contango - Futures price > Spot price
  • Backwardation - Futures price < Spot price

Futures Trading Strategies

  • Speculation - Betting on price direction
  • Hedging - Locking in future prices
  • Spread Trading - Trading price relationships
  • Arbitrage - Exploiting price inefficiencies

Risk Management

  • Margin Requirements - Initial and maintenance margin
    • Mark-to-Market - Daily profit/loss settlement
  • Position Limits - Maximum contract positions
  • Leverage Risk - High leverage amplifies losses

Swaps and Other Derivatives

Swaps are agreements to exchange cash flows based on different financial variables. They're primarily used for risk management and achieving specific financial objectives.

Interest Rate Swaps

  • Plain Vanilla - Fixed rate vs floating rate
  • Basis Swaps - Two floating rates
  • Currency Swaps - Different currencies
  • Amortizing Swaps - Notional amount decreases over time

Commodity Swaps

  • Fixed-for-Floating - Fixed price vs floating market price
  • Commodity-for-Interest - Commodity payments vs interest payments
  • Swaptions - Options on swaps

Credit Derivatives

  • Credit Default Swaps (CDS) - Insurance against default
  • Total Return Swaps - Exchange total return of assets
  • Credit Linked Notes - Bonds with embedded credit derivatives

Exotic Options

  • Asian Options - Payoff based on average price
  • Barrier Options - Activate or deactivate when barrier hit
  • Lookback Options - Best price over period
  • Binary Options - Fixed payoff if condition met
  • Chooser Options - Choose call or put later

Weather Derivatives

  • Temperature Swaps - Based on heating/cooling degree days
  • Precipitation Swaps - Based on rainfall amounts
  • Snowfall Swaps - Based on snow accumulation

Structured Products

  • Notes with Embedded Options - Bonds with option features
  • Guaranteed Principal Products - Capital protection with upside
  • Reverse Convertibles - High yield with equity risk

Risk Management in Derivatives

Derivatives risk management is critical due to leverage and complexity. Proper risk management prevents catastrophic losses and ensures long-term trading success.

Types of Risk

  • Market Risk - Price movements against position
  • Counterparty Risk - Default by trading partner
  • Liquidity Risk - Unable to exit position
  • Operational Risk - System failures, human error
  • Model Risk - Incorrect pricing models

Position Sizing

  • Portfolio Margin - Risk-based margin requirements
  • Value at Risk (VaR) - Maximum expected loss
  • Stress Testing - Extreme scenario analysis
  • Correlation Analysis - Position relationships

Greeks Management

  • Delta Neutral - Zero net delta exposure
  • Gamma Scalping - Trading gamma exposure
  • Theta Management - Balancing time decay
  • Vega Hedging - Managing volatility exposure

Stop Loss Strategies

  • Price-Based Stops - Exit at specific price levels
  • Time-Based Stops - Exit after time period
  • Volatility Stops - Adjust for market volatility
  • Greeks-Based Stops - Exit when Greeks exceed limits

Hedging Techniques

  • Delta Hedging - Maintain neutral delta
  • Cross-Hedging - Use related instruments
  • Dynamic Hedging - Continuous adjustment
  • Portfolio Insurance - Protect entire portfolio

Compliance and Regulation

  • Position Limits - Regulatory maximum positions
  • Reporting Requirements - Trade and position reporting
  • Capital Requirements - Minimum capital levels
  • Best Execution - Optimal trade execution

Trading Psychology and Discipline

Derivatives trading requires exceptional psychological discipline due to leverage, complexity, and rapid price movements. Mastering psychology is as important as understanding the instruments.

Psychological Challenges

  • Leverage Temptation - Overtrading due to high leverage
  • Complexity Overwhelm - Analysis paralysis
  • Quick Profit Expectations - Unrealistic get-rich-quick mentality
  • Fear of Missing Out (FOMO) - Chasing trades
  • Revenge Trading - Trying to recover losses quickly

Discipline Requirements

  • Strict Risk Management - Never risk more than planned
  • Patience - Wait for high-probability setups
  • Consistency - Follow trading plan religiously
  • Continuous Learning - Markets and products evolve

Trading Plan Essentials

  • Entry Criteria - Specific conditions for taking trades
  • Exit Rules - Profit targets and stop losses
  • Position Sizing - Risk per trade calculations
  • Review Process - Regular performance analysis

Emotional Control Techniques

  • Pre-Trade Preparation - Mental rehearsal
  • Mindfulness - Stay present and focused
  • Stress Management - Exercise, sleep, nutrition
  • Trading Journal - Track decisions and emotions

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overleveraging - Taking too large positions
  • Ignoring Greeks - Not understanding position risks
  • Chasing Losses - Increasing risk after losses
  • Complexity for Complexity's Sake - Using strategies without understanding
  • Blaming the Market - Not taking responsibility

Professional Development

  • Mentorship - Learn from experienced traders
  • Simulation Trading - Practice without real money
  • Continuous Education - Courses, books, seminars
  • Community - Network with other traders

Long-Term Success Factors

  • Survival over short-term profits
  • Risk management over returns
  • Process over outcomes
  • Discipline over talent
